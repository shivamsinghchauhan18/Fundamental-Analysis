# Module 1: The Core Tenets of Fundamental Analysis (Agent 1)

Traditional fundamental analysis uses a company's financial reports to calculate its intrinsic value. Speculative hype often inflates these metrics far beyond historical boundaries.

## 1.1 Trailing vs. Forward P/E (Price-to-Earnings) Ratio
The Price-to-Earnings ratio measures the price paid for a dollar of corporate earnings.
- **Mathematical Calculation:**
  $$\text{Trailing P/E} = \frac{\text{Current Share Price}}{\text{Earnings Per Share (EPS) over the Last 12 Months}}$$
  $$\text{Forward P/E} = \frac{\text{Current Share Price}}{\text{Projected EPS over the Next 12 Months}}$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - **Trailing P/E** represents a historical fact: what the company actually earned.
  - **Forward P/E** represents an analyst expectation: what the company is projected to earn.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - If a stock's Trailing P/E is extremely high (e.g., $160x$ for Palantir) but its Forward P/E is significantly lower ($85x$), the valuation is predicated entirely on an aggressive near-term earnings acceleration. If the company fails to hit those estimates, the multiple compresses violently, causing a severe drop in share price even if the company grew its profits.

---

## 1.2 Price-to-Sales (P/S) & EV/EBITDA Multiples
- **Mathematical Calculations:**
  $$\text{P/S Ratio} = \frac{\text{Market Capitalization}}{\text{Total Annual Sales (Revenue)}}$$
  $$\text{EV/EBITDA} = \frac{\text{Enterprise Value}}{\text{Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization (EBITDA)}}$$
  *Where Enterprise Value ($\text{EV}$) = Market Capitalization + Total Debt - Cash & Cash Equivalents.*
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - **P/S** measures the valuation relative to top-line revenue, ignoring profit margins.
  - **EV/EBITDA** measures valuation relative to cash operating profits, accounting for debt and cash structures.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - Growth stocks with extremely high P/S ratios ($6.8x$ for TSLA) indicate high premium pricing. High P/S multiples mean you are paying a high price for top-line revenue, which is only justified if the company can convert those sales into high-margin net income.

---

## 1.3 Gross, Operating, and Net Profit Margins
Margins measure a company's pricing power and operational efficiency.
- **Mathematical Calculations:**
  $$\text{Gross Margin \%} = \frac{\text{Gross Profit}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100$$
  $$\text{Operating Margin \%} = \frac{\text{Operating Income (EBIT)}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100$$
  $$\text{Net Margin \%} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - **Gross Margin** shows the efficiency of production before corporate overhead.
  - **Operating Margin** shows the profitability of the core business operations.
  - **Net Margin** is the bottom-line profitability after taxes, interest, and all other expenses.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - **Margin Compression Trend:** If a company's revenue is growing but its Gross Margin is compressing (as in Tesla's drop from $27.0\%$ to $17.8\%$), it indicates pricing pressure and eroding competitive advantage. Multiple expansion during margin compression is unsustainable; valuation must eventually align with lower-margin realities.

---

## 1.4 Free Cash Flow (FCF) Margin & CAGR
FCF is the actual cash generated by a business that is available to be returned to shareholders or reinvested.
- **Mathematical Calculations:**
  $$\text{Free Cash Flow (FCF)} = \text{Operating Cash Flow} - \text{Capital Expenditures (CapEx)}$$
  $$\text{FCF Margin \%} = \frac{\text{FCF}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100$$
  $$\text{FCF CAGR \%} = \left( \frac{\text{FCF}_{\text{End}}}{\text{FCF}_{\text{Start}}} \right)^{\frac{1}{n}} - 1 \times 100$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Net income can be manipulated by accounting rules. FCF represents the cold hard cash left over after maintaining the business's physical infrastructure.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - A high FCF Margin ($45.5\%$ for Nvidia) shows an incredibly profitable cash generator. A low or negative FCF margin during a multiple expansion cycle is a major red flag, indicating that the business requires continuous outside capital to survive.

---

## 1.5 Return on Equity (ROE) & Return on Assets (ROA)
These measure the efficiency with which a company allocates capital.
- **Mathematical Calculations:**
  $$\text{ROE \%} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Shareholders' Equity}} \times 100$$
  $$\text{ROA \%} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Total Assets}} \times 100$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - **ROE** shows how much profit is generated for every dollar of equity capital.
  - **ROA** shows how efficiently the company uses its total asset base.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - **Capital Efficiency:** High ROE ($72\%$ for Nvidia) shows exceptional capital efficiency. However, a high ROE can be artificially inflated by excessive leverage. Comparing it to ROA ($44.5\%$) verifies that the returns are driven by high profit margins and asset turnover rather than structural debt.

---

## 1.6 Capacity-Adjusted PEG (Price/Earnings-to-Growth) Ratio
The PEG ratio standardizes P/E by adjusting for the company's expected growth rate.
- **Mathematical Calculation:**
  $$\text{PEG Ratio} = \frac{\text{Trailing P/E}}{\text{Assumed Earnings Growth Rate CAGR (\%)}}$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - A PEG of **$1.0$** indicates a fair value: multiple matches expected growth (e.g., P/E of $25x$ for $25\%$ growth).
  - A PEG **$> 1.5$** represents overvaluation (you are paying too much for growth).
- **Investment Implication:**
  - Speculative growth stocks trade at bloated PEG multiples ($\text{PEG}_{\text{PLTR}} = 6.4$, $\text{PEG}_{\text{TSLA}} = 3.25$). This implies investors are paying a steep premium for expected growth. In our system, PEG is **capacity-adjusted**: if a company's expected growth is physically blocked by production ceilings, its true PEG is even higher.

# Module 2: Forward-Looking Operational Constraints (Agent 2)

Speculative models assume that growth can compound indefinitely. In the real world, growth is bounded by physical, operational, and supply chain constraints.

## 2.1 Installed Production Capacity & Global Utilization Rate
- **The Concept:**
  - **Installed Capacity:** The maximum theoretical volume a company can produce (e.g., 2,350,000 vehicles/year for Tesla).
  - **Utilization Rate:** The percentage of installed capacity actually being utilized.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - **The Physical Wall:** If Tesla's utilization rate is $85.2\%$, it can only expand production by another $14.8\%$ before requiring massive capital expenditures to build new factories. Speculative valuation models projecting $50\%$ vehicle volume growth CAGR are physically impossible without capital expansions that compress margins.

---

## 2.2 Order Book Lead Times & Backlog Days
- **The Concept:**
  - **Lead Time:** The time between customer order and actual product delivery.
  - **Backlog Days:** The volume of unrecognized revenue currently committed in the order book.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - A declining backlog (e.g., Tesla's drop from 45 days to 8 days) indicates that supply is outstripping demand, signaling inventory build-up and pressure to cut prices. Conversely, a healthy backlog (12-14 months for Nvidia's Blackwell GPUs) shows strong demand, but also reveals a supply constraint: revenue growth is capped by delivery capacity.

---

## 2.3 The Valuation Gap Equation
This metric quantifies the difference between what a company *must* earn to justify its current price and what it *can* realistically earn under operational limits.
- **Mathematical Calculation:**
  $$\text{Required Net Income} = \frac{\text{Market Capitalization}}{\text{Target P/E (justified by growth)}}$$
  $$\text{Required Revenue} = \frac{\text{Required Net Income}}{\text{Net Profit Margin}}$$
  $$\text{Valuation Gap} = \text{Required Revenue} - \text{Realistic Achievable Revenue (operational ceiling)}$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Solves for the revenue needed to justify today's valuation at a fair P/E multiple.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - **Tesla's Hope Premium:** Tesla's valuation implies it must generate **$411 Billion** in annual revenue to satisfy a fair valuation. Under realistic capacity limits, their maximum 2028 revenue is **$150 Billion**. The resulting **$261 Billion Valuation Gap** represents the premium priced in for non-operational narratives (Robotaxis, humanoid robots).

# Module 3: NLP Sentiment & Options Telemetry (Agent 3)

Asset bubbles are driven by retail narrative hype. Sentiment engine metrics allow us to track when retail sentiment is detaching from institutional fundamental flows.

## 3.1 NLP Media Sentiment & Sentiment Momentum Score
- **Mathematical Calculation (NLP word indexing):**
  $$\text{Sentiment Score} = \frac{\text{Count}(\text{Positive Words}) - \text{Count}(\text{Negative Words})}{\text{Count}(\text{Total Words})}$$
  $$\text{Sentiment Momentum} = \text{Sentiment Score} \times \text{Institutional Inflow Multiplier}$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Uses natural language processing (NLP) to index positive/negative media coverage.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - A highly typical pattern of retail bubbles is **media hype detachment**: positive sentiment index spikes (+0.72 for Nvidia) alongside high options leverage, while underlying operational capacity remains constrained.

---

## 3.2 Options Call/Put Volume Ratio & Short Interest
- **Mathematical Calculations:**
  $$\text{Call/Put Ratio} = \frac{\text{Volume of Call Options Traded}}{\text{Volume of Put Options Traded}}$$
  $$\text{Short Interest \%} = \frac{\text{Shares Sold Short}}{\text{Total Float (shares available to trade)}} \times 100$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - **Call/Put Ratio:** A high ratio ($>2.5$) indicates excessive bullish option leverage (speculators buying call options to amplify gains).
  - **Short Interest:** A high short interest ($>6\%$) indicates active bearish positioning.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - A bubbly call/put ratio ($3.1x$ for Nvidia) indicates a retail momentum peak. This options volume acts as a leverage accelerator: market makers must buy underlying stock to hedge their options delta, driving the price up artificially in a **gamma squeeze**. When option buying slows, this artificial demand vanishes, triggering a violent drop.

---

## 3.3 Net Insider Transactions
- **The Concept:**
  - Net volume of stock bought/sold by corporate executives and board members.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - **Smart Money Exit:** While retail sentiment is extremely positive, insiders aggressively selling their scheduled stock options ($1.8B sold for Nvidia, $840M for Palantir) is a major indicator. Insiders understand operational capacity limits; selling into retail hype indicates that corporate executives believe the stock is priced near its cyclical peak.

# Module 4: PEG-Adjusted DCF & Monte Carlo Simulators (Agent 4)

Standard valuations are static. To capture risk, we run dynamical simulations under varying interest rates and demand environments.

## 4.1 Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) Price Trajectories
GBM is the mathematical framework used to simulate future stock prices.
- **Mathematical Calculation:**
  $$dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dW_t$$
  Integrated as:
  $$S_t = S_0 \exp\left( \left( \mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right) dt + \sigma \sqrt{dt} Z_t \right)$$
  *Where $S_t$ is the stock price at step $t$, $\mu$ is the drift, $\sigma$ is the volatility, and $Z_t$ is a standard normal variable.*
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Models stock price as a continuous random walk with a deterministic drift and a stochastic (random) diffusion noise term.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - By simulating 10,000 future price paths, we get a complete probability distribution of where the stock will trade in two years. This allows us to quantify risk as a probability curve rather than a single point estimate.

---

## 4.2 Price Value-at-Risk (VaR 95%)
- **Mathematical Calculation:**
  $$\text{VaR}_{95\%} = S_0 - \text{5th Percentile Price of the Simulated GBM Distribution}$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Indicates that there is a $95\%$ probability the stock's maximum downside will not exceed this percentage over the horizon.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - A very high VaR ($46.2\%$ for Nvidia) shows that in a multiple compression cycle, there is a $5\%$ probability of experiencing a drop exceeding $46.2\%$, warning portfolio managers to hedge tail risks.

---

## 4.3 Stress Testing: Recession, Competition, and Margin Pressure
- **The Concept:**
  - Dynamic shocks applied to our cash flow and valuation models.
  - **Recession Stress:** growth drops to $5\%$, margins compress by $40\%$.
  - **Competition Stress:** growth drops by $40\%$, market share declines.
  - **Margin Pressure:** 500 bps drop in profit margins.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - Stress testing maps out downside outcomes. In our system, a competitive stress test compresses Tesla's fair value to **$40.61/share** ($81\%$ downside), showing how sensitive the valuation is to core market share erosion.

---

## 4.4 Required Breakeven Growth Period
- **Mathematical Calculation:**
  $$\text{Years Needed} = \frac{\log\left( \frac{\text{Current P/E}}{\text{Growth Rate Base}} \right)}{\log(1 + \text{Growth Rate Base})}$$
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Calculates the number of years a company must grow at its assumed rate to compress its trailing multiple down to its growth rate (PEG = 1.0).
- **Investment Implication:**
  - Palantir requires **11.4 years** of unconstrained $20\%$ growth CAGR to satisfy its P/E multiple at a PEG of 1.0. Given that enterprise software products rarely escape cyclical disruptions for over a decade, this breakeven period reveals how heavily the price relies on speculative assumptions.

# Module 5: Advanced Statistical Foundation (Agent 6)

Advanced statistical analysis audits the pricing data to ensure models are mathematically valid.

## 5.1 Asymmetric Return Distributions: Skewness & Kurtosis
- **Calculations:** See **Section 2.2**
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Measures the shape of the returns distribution.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - Growth stocks show **fat-tailed leptokurtosis** ($K > 3.0$). In a fat-tailed distribution, standard volatility metrics underestimate the frequency of extreme price drops, which can trigger sudden, severe losses.

---

## 5.2 Jarque-Bera Normality Test
- **Calculations:** See **Section 2.3**
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Tests whether stock returns conform to a standard normal distribution.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - Rejecting the normality hypothesis ($p < 0.05$) warns portfolio managers that traditional formulas assuming normal distributions are invalid. Position sizing must be adjusted for non-normal realities.

---

## 5.3 Dickey-Fuller Stationarity Test
- **Calculations:** See **Section 2.4**
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Audits price series for non-stationarity.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - Non-stationary prices pose a risk for trend-following models. To prevent **spurious regressions**, our system differences non-stationary prices into log-returns.

---

## 5.4 Autocorrelation Function (ACF)
- **Calculations:** See **Section 2.5**
- **Real-World Meaning:**
  - Identifies whether past returns predict future returns.
- **Investment Implication:**
  - ACF spikes reveal momentum or mean-reverting structures. A positive spike indicates a momentum effect (winners keep winning), while a negative spike indicates a mean-reverting structure.
